# 21 CFR Part 190 — New Dietary Ingredient (NDI) Screening

Part 190 governs the New Dietary Ingredient (NDI) notification process. The label reviewer's job is to **flag ingredients that may require an NDI notification** and recommend the brand confirm NDI status before launch.

## The rule — §190.6

Under 21 CFR 190.6, a manufacturer or distributor of a dietary supplement containing an NDI must submit a notification to FDA at least **75 days before** introducing the product into commerce, unless the NDI has been "present in the food supply as an article used for food in a form in which the food has not been chemically altered."

A **New Dietary Ingredient** is a dietary ingredient that was not marketed in the United States before **October 15, 1994** (DSHEA enactment date).

## What to check from the label

You usually cannot definitively determine NDI status from a label alone. The reviewer's job is to **screen for ingredients that warrant an NDI investigation** and flag them for the brand to confirm.

### Step 1 — List all active ingredients

From the Supplement Facts panel, list every active ingredient (vitamins, minerals, botanicals, amino acids, peptides, novel compounds).

### Step 2 — Classify each by NDI risk

**Low NDI risk** (likely grandfathered, pre-Oct 15 1994):
- Standard vitamins (A, B-complex, C, D, E, K) in conventional forms
- Standard minerals (calcium, iron, magnesium, zinc, etc.) in conventional salt forms
- Common amino acids (L-glutamine, L-lysine, BCAAs, L-theanine)
- Long-established botanicals (turmeric, ginger, echinacea, ginseng, ginkgo, milk thistle, valerian, etc.)
- Common extracts at conventional concentrations
- Common food ingredients (inulin, FOS, omega-3 from fish oil, etc.)

**Medium NDI risk** (may require confirmation):
- Branded/proprietary ingredient names (KSM-66®, Sensoril®, TheaCrine®, etc.) — these are typically self-affirmed GRAS or filed NDI; confirm the brand has documentation.
- Novel extract ratios or standardizations significantly different from traditional preparations.
- Specific isolated compounds from botanicals at amounts not historically consumed.

**High NDI risk** (almost certainly require NDI notification or are unlawful):
- **Synthetic peptides** — BPC-157, TB-500, semaglutide, tirzepatide, retatrutide. These are not lawful dietary ingredients; FDA has flagged BPC-157 as a Category 2 bulk drug substance (cannot be used in compounded medications), and synthetic GLP-1 analogs are FDA-approved drugs, not supplements.
- **Methylene Blue** — pharmaceutical-grade methylene blue is a drug; cosmetic/industrial-grade is not GRAS for supplements. FDA scrutiny ongoing.
- **NAD+, NMN, NR** — NMN status is contested (FDA notified industry in 2022 that NMN cannot be marketed as a supplement because it has been "authorized for investigation as a new drug"); NAD+ and NR have their own regulatory histories. Check current FDA position.
- **CBD** — FDA position is that CBD is not a lawful dietary ingredient because it was first studied as a drug (Epidiolex).
- **Kratom (Mitragyna speciosa)** — FDA has not approved kratom for any use; multiple warning letters; some states ban it.
- **Higenamine, DMAA, DMHA, Tianeptine** — stimulants/synthetics flagged by FDA in warning letters.
- **Synthetic THC analogs (Delta-8, Delta-10, HHC, etc.)** — not lawful dietary ingredients; Farm Bill loophole is contested.

### Step 3 — Flag findings

For each medium- or high-risk ingredient:
- **Critical** — High-risk ingredient is present (e.g., methylene blue, synthetic peptide, CBD, NMN under current FDA position). Cannot lawfully ship.
- **Major** — Medium-risk ingredient is present without indication that NDI documentation exists.
- **Minor** — Branded ingredients (KSM-66, Sensoril, etc.) — typically have NDI/GRAS docs, but confirm.

## Common NDI questions for the brand

When flagging, ask the brand to confirm:
1. Is this ingredient grandfathered (in food supply pre-Oct 15, 1994)?
2. If not, has the brand or supplier filed an NDI notification with FDA?
3. Is there a successful NDI notification on file (FDA accepted, not "no further action")?
4. Are there current FDA warning letters or guidance documents flagging this ingredient?

## Quick pass criteria

| Item | Pass criteria | Citation |
|---|---|---|
| NDI screen — all actives | No high-risk ingredients present | 21 CFR 190.6 |
| NDI documentation | Medium-risk ingredients have NDI notification or grandfather support documented | 21 CFR 190.6 |
| Branded ingredients | Documentation on file for proprietary extracts | 21 CFR 190.6 |
