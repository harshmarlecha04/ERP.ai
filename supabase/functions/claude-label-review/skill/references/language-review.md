# Language Review — Spelling, Grammar, Punctuation, Symbols

This reference covers the **non-regulatory** editorial review portion of label review. Findings here are **informational** and **not part of the CFR findings**. They go in a separate "Language Review" section of the report.

The goal is to catch the kinds of errors that genuinely embarrass a brand on shelf or that signal sloppy QA — not to do a full proofread of every comma.

## 1. Spelling — supplement industry vocabulary

The dietary supplement category has its own vocabulary that often trips up copywriters and designers. Common pitfalls:

### Botanicals (often misspelled)
- Ashwagandha (not Ashwaganda)
- Rhodiola rosea (not Rhodiala)
- Bacopa monnieri (not Bacopa monneri)
- Ginkgo biloba (not Gingko)
- Schisandra chinensis (not Schizandra)
- Withania somnifera, Centella asiatica, Boswellia serrata, Eleutherococcus senticosus

### Nutrients / Forms
- Acetyl-L-Carnitine (hyphenated; not "Acetyl L Carnitine")
- Phosphatidylserine (one word)
- Pyridoxal-5-phosphate (P-5-P)
- Cyanocobalamin / Methylcobalamin (B12 forms)
- Cholecalciferol (D3) / Ergocalciferol (D2)
- Phylloquinone (K1) / Menaquinone (K2 / MK-4 / MK-7)
- L-5-methyltetrahydrofolate (5-MTHF)

### Compound / Combined Words
- Trisodium Citrate (one word, not "Tri Sodium")
- Bioavailability / Bioavailable (one word)
- Antioxidant (one word)
- Polyphenol(s)
- Multivitamin

### Chemical formulas (case-sensitive)
- HCl (hydrochloride) — lowercase L, not capital I. OCR commonly confuses these.
- HBr, HNO3, H2SO4 — preserve exact case and subscripts
- pH (lowercase p, capital H)
- CoQ10 (capital C, lowercase o, capital Q)
- NAD+, NMN, NR — preserve case

## 2. Punctuation

- **Parenthesis spacing** — always a space before `(` (e.g., "Pectin (from Citrus & Apple)" not "Pectin(from...").
- **Em-dash vs hyphen** — em-dash (—) for parenthetical breaks; hyphen (-) for compounds. Designers often use hyphens where em-dashes belong.
- **En-dash vs hyphen** — en-dash (–) for ranges ("ages 18–65"); hyphen for compounds ("18-year-old").
- **Oxford comma** — be consistent within a single label. Mixing Oxford and non-Oxford styles within one panel is sloppy.
- **Quotation marks** — curly quotes (" ") preferred over straight (" ") in print.

---

## 3. Capitalization

- **Vitamins**: capital V (Vitamin C, Vitamin D); units lowercase (mg, mcg, IU).
- **Botanical Latin names**: italicized; genus capitalized, species lowercase (*Curcuma longa*, *Withania somnifera*).
- **Brand names**: follow the brand's chosen capitalization (e.g., TheHEME® stays "TheHEME").
- **"Non-GMO"**: capital G-M-O; "Gluten-Free" hyphenated.
- **"All Natural"**: best practice not to capitalize (it's not an FDA-regulated term).

## 4. Trademark / Copyright Symbols

Three symbols, three uses:

| Symbol | Meaning | Use |
|---|---|---|
| ® | Registered trademark | Federally registered with USPTO. Use after the mark on first/most prominent occurrence per panel. |
| ™ | Unregistered trademark | Mark claimed but not federally registered. Use when registration is pending or not pursued. |
| © | Copyright | Used in copyright notice with year and owner ("© 2026 [Brand]. All rights reserved."). |

**Common failures:**
- Missing ® on ingredient brands (KSM-66, Bioperine, etc.) where the trademark owner requires it.
- Using ® on a mark that's not federally registered — use ™ until registration completes.
- Missing © in the copyright notice (e.g., "2026 Lunakai® All rights reserved" reads as if the *brand* was registered in 2026; should be "© 2026 Lunakai. All rights reserved.").
- Symbol formatted as full-size text rather than superscript.

## 5. Common label-specific style issues

### Numbers and units
- Numerals for measurements (30 mg, not "thirty mg").
- Don't mix mg with "milligrams" in the same panel.
- IU is being phased out by FDA for Vitamins A, D, E (post-2016 NLEA); newer labels should use mcg or mg.

### Compound modifiers
- Hyphenate before a noun ("third-party tested ingredients").
- No hyphen after a noun ("the ingredients are third party tested").

### Abbreviations
- Spell out on first use, abbreviate after ("Coenzyme Q10 (CoQ10)... CoQ10...").
- Common abbreviations don't need spelling out: mg, mcg, IU, oz, fl oz, g.

## What to flag and how

For each language issue, capture:
- **What/where** on the label
- **Issue** (one sentence describing the error)
- **Recommended fix** (the corrected text)

Severity is not used for language items — they're all reported as one tier in the "Language Review" section. If something is truly trivial (e.g., one missing serial comma in a long sentence on the back panel), skip it. Focus on errors that a normal reader would notice or that compromise the brand's professionalism.

**Suspected OCR / text-extraction artifacts:** When reviewing extracted PDF text, watch for:
- l ↔ I confusion ("HCI" should likely be "HCl")
- 0 ↔ O confusion in product codes
- Spacing errors that may be PDF text-extraction artifacts ("FACIL TI Y")

For suspected OCR artifacts, flag the issue and add "verify on press-ready art" — don't assume it's a real label error without visual confirmation.
