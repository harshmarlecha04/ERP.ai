# 21 CFR 101.17 — Warning Statements & Required Cautions

This reference covers the **mandated warning statements** that must appear on certain dietary supplement labels. Unlike claims (101.93) or panel format (101.36), §101.17 warnings are **ingredient-triggered** — they only apply when specific ingredients are present at specific levels.

The single most common failure in label review is the **opposite** of you'd expect: reviewers invent warnings that aren't actually required. Be precise. Only flag a missing warning when the specific FDA regulation actually mandates it for an ingredient present in the formula.

---

## 1. FDA-mandated warnings — ingredient-specific

These warnings are **required by federal regulation** when the triggering ingredient is present. If the ingredient is in the formula and the warning is missing, flag as **Critical**.

### 1.1 Iron supplements — §101.17(e)

If the product contains **≥30 mg of elemental iron per serving** (or the package contains products with iron), the label must bear:

> "**WARNING: Accidental overdose of iron-containing products is a leading cause of fatal poisoning in children under 6. Keep this product out of reach of children. In case of accidental overdose, call a doctor or poison control center immediately.**"

Specific formatting requirements apply (boxed, bold "WARNING").

### 1.2 Psyllium husk — §101.17(d)

If the product contains **dry or incompletely hydrated psyllium husk** intended to be consumed in dry form, the label must bear a choking warning, typically:

> "**NOTICE: This product should be taken with at least a full glass of liquid. Taking this product without enough liquid may cause choking. Do not take this product if you have difficulty swallowing.**"

### 1.3 Aspartame — 21 CFR 172.804

Products containing aspartame must include:

> "**PHENYLKETONURICS: CONTAINS PHENYLALANINE.**"

(Aspartame is rare in supplements but does appear in some sugar-free gummies and chewables.)

### 1.4 Sorbitol — 21 CFR 184.1835(e)

If a product's reasonably foreseeable daily consumption could result in ≥50 g of sorbitol, the label must bear:

> "**Excess consumption may have a laxative effect.**"

### 1.5 Other ingredient-specific warnings to verify

- **Caffeine** — no FDA-mandated warning, but products with high caffeine often voluntarily include one.
- **Yohimbe / yohimbine** — FDA has issued warnings; voluntary cautionary language is industry standard but not strictly mandated.
- **Ephedra** — banned in dietary supplements since 2004 (§119.1). If present at all, the product is unlawful — flag as Critical.
- **Comfrey (oral) / pyrrolizidine alkaloids** — FDA advised against in 2001; voluntary warnings common.

## 2. Industry-standard warnings — NOT FDA-mandated

These warnings are present on most supplement labels because of brand practice, retailer requirements, and liability management — **not because §101.17 requires them**. Don't flag their absence as a CFR violation; you can note them as a Minor "industry best practice" item if relevant.

### 2.1 "Keep Out of Reach of Children" (KOROC)

Standard on nearly every supplement, but FDA does NOT mandate it under §101.17 except for the iron warning context (§1.1 above). It's required by some state laws and retailer specs. Industry-standard placement is in the CAUTION block.

### 2.2 Pregnancy / nursing warning

The phrase "If pregnant or nursing, consult a healthcare professional before use" is industry-standard but is NOT mandated under §101.17 for the general supplement category. It IS mandated for specific ingredients (e.g., the iron warning context implicitly addresses pediatric exposure; some botanicals have FDA guidance but not a §101.17 mandate).

**Don't invent a missing "pregnancy warning" finding for ingredients FDA has not specifically required it for.** Common overreach:
- Berberine — clinical literature notes hyperbilirubinemia/kernicterus risk in neonates, but FDA has NOT issued a §101.17 mandate. Industry voluntarily includes pregnancy warnings; absence is not a CFR violation. At most, note as a Minor industry-best-practice item.
- Turmeric / curcumin — same.
- Garcinia cambogia — same.

### 2.3 Drug-interaction warnings

"Consult your physician if taking medication" is industry-standard but not §101.17-mandated.

### 2.4 General medical-condition warning

"If you have a medical condition, consult a healthcare professional before use" — industry-standard, not mandated.

---

## 3. Warning statement formatting

Where a warning IS required, it must be:

- **Prominent and conspicuous** under §101.15(a)
- Often required to be **boxed** and in **bold** (per the specific warning's regulation)
- In type size at least 1/16 inch (or 6-point) for small packages; larger for bigger packages

**Common failure:** Iron warning present but not boxed or not in bold. Flag as Major under §101.17(e)(2).

---

## 4. Severity calibration for §101.17 findings

- **Critical** — Missing FDA-mandated warning for an ingredient that is in the formula (iron, psyllium, aspartame, sorbitol over threshold).
- **Major** — Required warning present but improperly formatted (not boxed, not bold, wrong wording).
- **Minor** — Industry-standard warning missing where there's no FDA mandate (KOROC, general pregnancy warning, drug-interaction caveat). Only flag if Pharmvista or the client explicitly wants industry-best-practice findings.

**Never flag:**
- Missing pregnancy warning for Berberine, Turmeric, Garcinia, or any other botanical where no FDA mandate exists.
- Missing "consult your doctor" line — industry standard, not a CFR violation.
- A warning that the brand chose not to include but federal regulation doesn't require.

---

## 5. Quick checklist

- [ ] Iron content checked: if ≥30 mg elemental Fe/serving, iron warning present and properly boxed/bolded? (§101.17(e))
- [ ] Psyllium present? If yes, choking warning present? (§101.17(d))
- [ ] Aspartame present? If yes, PKU warning present? (§172.804)
- [ ] Sorbitol present at threshold? If yes, laxative warning? (§184.1835)
- [ ] Ephedra present? If yes, product is unlawful (§119.1)
- [ ] All mandatory warnings properly formatted (bold, boxed, prominent)
- [ ] Don't invent warnings — verify each finding cites an actual mandate
