# 21 CFR 101.93 — Statements Made for Dietary Supplements

This is the only section of Part 101 in scope (besides 101.36). It governs claims on dietary supplement labels and the DSHEA disclaimer.

## §101.93(b)–(d) — DSHEA Disclaimer

If the label bears any structure/function claim, this exact disclaimer is required:

> **"These statements have not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."**

**Verify:**
- Present whenever any structure/function claim is on the label.
- Linked to the claim via asterisk (*) or symbol per §101.93(d).
- Type ≥ 1/16 inch x-height.
- Wording exactly as required — do not modify.

**Common failures:**
- Disclaimer present but no asterisk linking to the claim.
- Disclaimer wording altered (e.g., dropping "by the Food and Drug Administration").
- Claim on PDP but disclaimer only on back panel with no symbol linkage.
- Disclaimer absent entirely on a label that makes S/F claims.

---

## §101.93(f) — Structure/Function Claims (allowed)

Claims describing the role of a nutrient/ingredient in the normal **structure or function** of the body. These are allowed if:
- The brand has substantiation files on hand.
- The brand notifies FDA within 30 days of first marketing the claim.
- The DSHEA disclaimer (§101.93(b)–(d)) is present.

**The compliant pattern:** support / maintain / promote / help + healthy / normal + structure or function.

**Compliant examples:**
- "Supports a healthy immune system"
- "Promotes restful sleep"
- "Helps maintain healthy cholesterol levels already within the normal range"
- "Supports cognitive function"
- "Helps maintain healthy energy levels"
- "Supports joint comfort"
- "Supports a healthy inflammatory response"
- "Helps maintain healthy blood sugar levels already within the normal range"

---

## §101.93(g) — Disease Claims (NOT allowed)

A "disease claim" is a claim that the product:
- Diagnoses, treats, cures, mitigates, or prevents a disease.
- Treats characteristic signs or symptoms of a specific disease.
- Affects an abnormal condition of the body that constitutes a disease.

Disease claims convert a dietary supplement into an unapproved drug. They are not allowed regardless of whether the DSHEA disclaimer is present.

### Red-flag verbs (almost always disease claims)
- treats, treatment, treating
- cures, curing
- prevents, prevention, preventing
- diagnoses, diagnosing
- mitigates, mitigating
- heals, healing
- remedies, remedy
- reduces (in disease context, e.g., "reduces blood pressure")
- lowers (in disease context, e.g., "lowers cholesterol")
- relieves (in disease-symptom context)

### Red-flag disease names
Any reference to a named disease or condition is a disease claim:
- diabetes, blood sugar control (borderline — depends on framing)
- hypertension, high blood pressure
- arthritis, osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis
- cancer, tumor
- depression, anxiety (clinical)
- Alzheimer's, dementia, cognitive decline
- insomnia
- ADD/ADHD
- IBS, Crohn's, colitis
- obesity, "weight loss" (FDA scrutinizes weight-loss claims closely)
- migraine
- erectile dysfunction

### Implicit disease claims — the gray zone

Disease claims can be implicit, not just explicit. FDA considers the entire label context — imagery, product name, dosing context, even brand social media. But the regulatory bar between "clear violation" and "elevated risk" matters for severity grading.

**Key principle for hormone/biological-target references:** A naturally occurring hormone or biological target (GLP-1, dopamine, cortisol, melatonin, etc.) is **not solely associated with a disease**. References to these targets — including in product names — **can be used as part of a structure/function claim** if the claim describes the role in normal body function. The line is crossed when the label compares to, claims equivalence with, or claims to replace/enhance an FDA-approved drug.

**Severity calibration for claim language:**

| Pattern | Typical severity | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Explicit disease verb + named disease** ("treats diabetes," "lowers cholesterol," "prevents Alzheimer's," "cures insomnia") | Critical | Unambiguous §101.93(g) violation. Converts supplement to unapproved drug. |
| **Direct drug comparison or equivalence** ("natural Ozempic," "same as Wegovy," "alternative to statins," "as effective as Lipitor") | Critical | FDA position: products promising to replace, serve as an alternative to, or enhance the efficacy/safety of approved drugs are unlawful. Veronvy warning letter (Dec 2024) cited exactly this — comparing the supplement to semaglutide. |
| **Claim to "boost," "replace," or "enhance" an FDA-approved drug's effect** ("boosts your Ozempic," "use alongside semaglutide for better results") | Critical | Same principle as above. |
| **Disease imagery + product** (inflamed joint, anatomy diagrams of disease state) | Critical | Visual disease association reads as a treatment claim. |
| **Hormone or biological-target reference in product name** ("GLP-1 Support," "Cortisol Calm," "Dopamine Boost") with proper DSHEA disclaimer, S/F verb ("support"), and **no drug comparison** | **Not a finding** | Per FDA/regulatory commentary: naturally occurring hormones are not solely associated with disease and can be referenced in S/F claims. Risk exists from drug-manufacturer C&D letters and class action litigation, but this is a business risk, not a CFR violation. |
| **Disease-class name in product name** ("DiaBetic Support," "Arthritis Relief," "Insomnia Cure") | Critical | Uses an actual disease name, not just a biological target. |
| **Borderline S/F language** ("supports healthy blood pressure" without "already within the normal range") | Major | Easy fix; not a Critical violation but should be tightened. |
| **Compliant S/F language** ("supports immune function," "promotes restful sleep," "helps maintain healthy [X] already within the normal range") | Pass | The safe-harbor pattern. |

**What this means in practice:** Don't flag "GLP-1 Support," "Cortisol Calm," "Dopamine Boost," or similar biological-target framings as CFR violations on their own. Look at the broader label for the actual disqualifiers — drug comparisons, disease names, equivalence claims, quantitative drug-mimicking claims. If those are absent, the framing is within DSHEA's S/F safe harbor regardless of what the headline product name suggests.

**When the framing tips to Critical:**
- Product name + drug name ("GLP-1 Support — Like Ozempic, but natural").
- Product name + explicit disease ("GLP-1 Support for Diabetes").
- Product name + quantitative drug-mimicking claims ("clinically shown to suppress appetite by 47%").
- Product name + claim to replace/augment the drug ("Skip the injection — try our GLP-1 Support").

## Worked example — "GLP-1 Support" naming on a gummy supplement

This is a tricky case that's easy to over-flag. Use this as a calibration check whenever a label uses a hormone, neurotransmitter, or drug-target name in its product identity.

### The setup
A gummy supplement labeled "GLP-1 SUPPORT*" on the PDP, with:
- An asterisk linking to the DSHEA disclaimer (present and verbatim).
- The verb "support" used (not "treat," "cure," "boost," "replace").
- Ingredients: berberine, garcinia, green tea, resveratrol, raspberry ketone, etc.
- No mention of Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro, semaglutide, tirzepatide.
- No mention of diabetes, obesity, or weight loss as a disease.
- No "as good as" or "natural alternative to" language.
- No quantitative drug-mimicking claims ("suppresses appetite by X%").

### The naive (wrong) analysis
"GLP-1 is the mechanism of FDA-approved drugs for diabetes and obesity → using 'GLP-1' in the product name implies treatment of those conditions → implicit disease claim under §101.93(g) → Critical or Major finding."

### Why that's wrong
GLP-1 is **a naturally occurring hormone in the body**, not a disease. The legal framework: a reference to a normally occurring biological target can be used as part of a structure/function claim if the claim describes the role of the target in normal body function. The product is not converted into an unapproved drug merely because the same hormone is also the mechanism of an approved drug.

### What FDA actually targets — the Veronvy case
The Veronvy warning letter (Dec 10, 2024), which is sometimes cited as a GLP-1-supplement precedent, was actually about products named "Elily Veronvy" and "Elily Veronvy 40+" that were:
- Marketed online as **"oral GLP-1 treatments"** (explicit drug-claim language — "treatments").
- **Falsely claiming FDA approval**.
- Compared directly to the FDA-approved injectables.

Those acts — the treatment claim, the FDA-approval claim, the drug comparison — are what made Veronvy unlawful. **Not the use of "GLP-1" in the product name.**

### Correct severity call for the gummy in the setup above
**Not a CFR finding.** Within DSHEA's S/F safe harbor.

### What WOULD tip this into a Critical CFR finding
If the same gummy added any of the following:
- "Natural Ozempic" or "Like Wegovy, but better" → drug comparison → Critical.
- "GLP-1 Support for Diabetes" → disease name → Critical.
- "Clinically proven to suppress appetite by 47%" → quantitative drug-mimicking → Critical.
- "Use alongside your GLP-1 medication for better results" → augmenting an approved drug → Critical.
- "An alternative to weekly injections" → claim to replace the drug → Critical.

### What to do with the litigation risk
Drug manufacturers (Novo, Lilly) routinely send cease-and-desist letters to supplement brands using GLP-1 framing, and the Lemme class action is the active litigation example. **These are business risks, not CFR violations.** They are outside the scope of this skill and should not be flagged as Critical or Major findings. If the user wants to know about them, mention them in the chat summary as context — not in the report.

### Generalizing — other hormone/target product names

Same principle applies to other biological-target framings:

| Product name pattern | Severity (with proper DSHEA + S/F verb, no drug comparison) |
|---|---|
| "Cortisol Calm" | Not a finding (cortisol is a hormone; "calm" is S/F language) |
| "Dopamine Boost" | Not a finding |
| "Serotonin Support" | Not a finding |
| "Melatonin Sleep" | Not a finding |
| "GABA Calm" | Not a finding |
| "Testosterone Support" | Not a finding (hormone reference; common category) |

But these tip to **Critical**:

| Product name pattern | Why |
|---|---|
| "Cortisol Cure" | Verb tied to disease state |
| "Dopamine Therapy" | Drug-class verb |
| "Serotonin Antidepressant" | Drug-class reference |
| "Natural SSRI" | Direct drug comparison |
| "GLP-1 for Diabetes" | Disease name |
| "Testosterone Replacement" | Claims to replace a prescription therapy |
